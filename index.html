TamTam · Mango + Tamarind
/03 — TAMTAM

Some flavors you discover.
Some you remember.

In Nigeria, tamarind is called awin — or licky licky, for the way children eat it straight from the pod, tart and sweet and completely addictive. During its season, our mom made sure we had a steady supply. Mango was the same — unavoidable, generous, everywhere you turned.

TamTam is those two fruits together. Not as a novelty. As a memory.

Lazy afternoons. Sticky fingers. The kind of simple joy that stays with you.

Sweet mango. Tart tamarind. A hint of lemon sharpens it all.
Chapman · Spiced Citrus
/01 — CHAPMAN

Not every moment is special.
Chapman disagrees.

In Nigeria, Chapman is the drink that signals the occasion is real. It appears at every Owambe — Lagos's legendary celebrations where the food is abundant, the music is loud, and nobody leaves early.

It is the drink the vendor makes fresh for guests at the door. It is what you ordered at a fancy restaurant as a child and felt, for a moment, like the most important person in the room.

Some drinks mark the moment. Chapman has always been the moment.

Citrus forward. Cooling cucumber. A hint of spice.
Obì · Kola Bitter
/02 — OBI

Some drinks arrive at the table.
Obì has always been the table.

In Yoruba, obì is the kola nut — offered to guests as a symbol of respect and welcome. In Igbo, it speaks to leadership and community. Two languages, one shared idea.

Growing up in Nigeria, my grandmother always had obì ready for special guests. I would watch from a distance — the ceremony of it, the weight it carried, the way it transformed an ordinary visit into something significant. I was too young to participate. So I watched. And I wanted, more than anything, to understand what it meant to be welcomed that way.

Years later, as an adult, I was invited to the home of the late Chief Joseph Chike Edozien — the Asagba of Asaba, the King of Asaba, Delta State, Nigeria. And for the first time in my life, I was the one being served obì.

The child who watched from the doorway had become the guest at the table.

That feeling — of being truly welcomed, of belonging, of a tradition wrapping itself around you — is what Obì, the drink, is built to carry.

Bold kola. Layered bitters. Blood orange cuts through.

Obì is not just a flavor. It is an invitation. A gesture that says: you belong here. The table is set. The celebration is real.

Zobo · Hibiscus + Ginger
/04 — ZOBO

Some drinks follow.
Zobo leads.

The first sip I ever had of zobo came from a street hawker walking home from school in Lagos. My siblings and I pooled our courage — and whatever change we had — and bought a cold cup between us. That was it. Love at first sip.

Our mother saw what had happened to us and bought enough zobo (hibiscus) leaves to last a month. They were gone in a week.

That is zobo. It does not ease you in. It takes over completely. Born in Nigerian kitchens, loved across the continent.

Tart hibiscus. Bright heat of ginger. The warming depth of clove.

The founder
Zainob Fashola

"I grew up with these flavors."

Zainob Fashola · Founder of ZZAI

My name is Zainob Fashola. I've spent 18 years in product development and consumer brands — building, launching, and running products from concept to shelf across operations, quality, and go-to-market.

ZZAI is personal.

I grew up making Zobo at home; hibiscus, ginger, clove, steeped and sweetened and poured for anyone who walked through the door. I grew up watching kola nut passed at gatherings, carried with the kind of weight that tells you something important is about to happen. Chapman was at every celebration worth remembering.

These aren't flavors I researched. They're flavors I come from.

What I couldn't find was a version of them that belonged on a modern shelf — in a can, carbonated, next to the brands I'd spent my career helping build. So I made it.

ZZAI is four sparkling beverages rooted in West African flavor tradition. Not inspired by it. Rooted in it. Real cane sugar. No artificial anything. And a cultural figure on every can whose story is as specific as the flavor it carries.

This is my next chapter. I'd like it to be yours too.

— Zainob Fashola, Founder
